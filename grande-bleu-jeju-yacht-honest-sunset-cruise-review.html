Why this one, not the others

Most Jeju boat tours lean into volume — pile everyone on, hand out life jackets, blast the speakers. Grande Bleu goes the other way. It's the only yacht in Jeju where life jackets aren't required onboard, which sounds like a small detail until you're trying to take photos in soft golden-hour light without a fluorescent orange vest in every frame.

The boat itself is a proper catamaran — twin hulls, 17m, around 40 tons — so it sits flat on the water. No lurching, no clinging to the rail. You can walk the deck with a glass of wine and not spill it. For a corporate friends' trip where half the group will inevitably wear something they shouldn't wear on a normal boat, that matters.

Grande Bleu has been running since 2011, has served over 100,000 guests, and partners with Shilla Hotel and Parnas Jeju. That's the kind of background you want when you're booking on behalf of colleagues.

The route: 60 minutes of Jeju's volcanic coast

You leave Daepo Port and curve along the Seogwipo coastline — the dramatic bit, not the touristy harbour bit. In order:

  • Elephant Rock (Kkokkiribawi) — a basalt cliff that genuinely looks like an elephant drinking
  • Jingotnae Sea Canyon — vertical cliff walls only visible from the water
  • Wolpyeong Basalt Columns — hexagonal volcanic pillars, part of Jeju's UNESCO Global Geopark geology
  • Wolpyeong Sea Cave and the Large Sea Cave (Keunbada Donggul) — the biggest on this stretch
  • Pangpaengideok — a wide flat reef plateau that looks unreal at low sun

In autumn (September to November), the light hits these cliffs at a low angle and the basalt turns warm rust-red against navy water. Compared with Bali sunset cruises (busier, hazier) or the Greek islands (longer, pricier, further), Jeju in October gives you cleaner air and a tighter, more cinematic hour.

What's actually free onboard (and it's a lot)

This is where the ROI calculation tips. Everything below is unlimited and included in the ticket price — no hidden bar tab, no upsell:

  • Korean draft beer (fresh brew)
  • Red and white wine
  • Jeju tangerine juice, fresh-squeezed
  • Bottled water
  • Local Jeju snacks and cookies
  • Korean ramyeon, cooked onboard

For a group of four or five, the drinks alone justify the ticket. Order two glasses of wine at any Jeju resort bar and you're already past the cruise price.

Fishing gear is also free. You can drop a line for Jeju rockfish, scorpionfish, filefish or puffer — beginner-friendly, catch-and-release if you prefer. It's a five-minute novelty, not a serious fishing trip, but it photographs well.

What nobody tells you

A few small things that make the difference:

  • Book the second-to-last departure, not the final one. You want the sun *during* the cruise, not setting as you dock.
  • October sunset in Jeju is around 17:30–18:00 — confirm on the Klook timetable before booking flights back.
  • Bring a light jacket. The deck gets breezy once the sun drops, even in early autumn.
  • Phones, not cameras. Modern phones handle golden hour better than most travel cameras, and you don't want a strap flapping in the wind.
  • Korean phone support only (+82-64-739-7776). For English, everything goes through Klook chat — which is actually faster.
