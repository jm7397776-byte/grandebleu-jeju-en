Why a Catamaran Beats a Mono-Hull (the boring but useful bit)

Most Jeju "yacht tours" run on single-hull boats. Grande Bleu runs a twin-hull catamaran, and the difference is physical, not marketing.

Two hulls spread the load, so lateral rocking drops by roughly 90%. In practice: you can hold a wine glass without bracing, kids don't go green, and your photos aren't blurry from the deck pitching.

For winter sailing in Jeju, that stability matters more than in summer. Seas are choppier December through February, and a mono-hull will let you know about it.

The Route: 60 Minutes Along Seogwipo's Coast

The course traces the south Jeju coastline — part of a UNESCO Global Geopark zone — and hits six landmarks in one loop.

  • Wolpyeong Basalt Columns — hexagonal volcanic cliffs, the textbook Jeju geology shot
  • Wolpyeong Sea Cave — the boat eases to about 8m off the entrance; you'll hear waves echoing inside
  • Elephant Rock (Kkokkiribawi) — the wind picks up here; the trunk shape is obvious from sea level
  • Jingotnae Sea Canyon — vertical cliffs that block the wind, suddenly quiet water
  • Pangpaengideok — a wide flat reef plateau, the easiest spot for clean wide-angle shots
  • Keunbada Donggul — the biggest sea cave on this stretch of coast

Morning light hits the basalt columns sideways and exaggerates the shadows. Sunset gives you the 360-degree golden hour — happens once a day, runs about 20 minutes.

Fishing: Yes, You'll Probably Catch Something

The fishing leg lands you over real Jeju waters, not a stocked tank. Common catches: rockfish, scorpionfish, filefish, and the occasional puffer. Gear is provided, no licence needed, catch-and-release is fine.

It's beginner-level. Kids tend to lock in; adults tend to loosen up. Both can happen on the same deck without anyone getting in the way — that's what the 91-guest capacity buys you.

Winter Sailing in Jeju: What to Know

Jeju's winter sits between 5–12°C — colder than Bali, much warmer than Seoul, and the southern coast around Seogwipo is the warmest pocket on the island. Sailings run year-round.

What to bring on a December–February cruise:

  • A windbreaker or shell layer (the wind on deck cuts harder than the air temp suggests)
  • Sunglasses — low winter sun off the water is brutal
  • A proper camera if you have one; phone wide-angle works for the basalt columns
  • Closed shoes; the teak deck is fine but spray happens

There's a fully enclosed indoor salon if the wind gets serious. Korea's top-tier safety certification means you can ride on deck without a life vest — which is why the photos actually look like you're sailing, not safety drilling.
