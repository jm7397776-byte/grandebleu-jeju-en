Why a Catamaran in Jeju, Not Bali?

Honest answer: Jeju isn't trying to be the Maldives, and Grande Bleu isn't pretending to be a superyacht in Monaco. What you get instead is a stable twin-hull catamaran — the only certified one in Korea — sailing a 60-minute loop along volcanic cliffs that most visitors only ever see from a roadside viewpoint.

Compared with the Greek islands or Phuket, the route is short and the price is low. For couples in their 40s who'd rather not commit half a day to a boat, that's the point. You sail, you eat, you get the photos, and you're back at your Shilla or Parnas suite before the kids hit meltdown.

The Route: Cliffs You Can't See From the Road

The 60-minute course covers a stretch of UNESCO-grade volcanic coast you genuinely can't reach by car:

  • Elephant Rock (Kkokkiribawi) — a natural cliff shaped like an elephant's head
  • Jingotnae Sea Canyon — vertical cliff walls dropping straight into the sea
  • Wolpyeong Basalt Columns — hexagonal volcanic columns, part of the Jeju UNESCO Geopark
  • Wolpyeong Sea Cave — dark entrance, deeper blue water inside
  • Pangpaengideok — a wide flat reef plateau, the most photogenic stop
  • Keunbada Donggul (Large Sea Cave) — the biggest cave on this coast

There's a viewpoint above the basalt columns you can drive to, but it's a top-down angle. From the water, you look up at them — same geology, completely different photo.

Food and Drinks: Genuinely Free, Genuinely Unlimited

This is where the value math works out. Onboard, all of this is included with no extra charge and unlimited refills:

  • Korean draft beer (fresh brew)
  • Red and white wine
  • Fresh-squeezed Jeju tangerine juice
  • Bottled water
  • Jeju local cookies and traditional snacks
  • Korean ramyeon, cooked onboard

It's not a tasting menu. But a hot cup of ramyeon at sea level with a glass of wine in the other hand is, oddly, the bit most guests remember.

Fishing — Yes, Even on a Sunset Cruise

Rods and bait are provided free, and the crew shows you how to use them. Common catches in this stretch of water are rockfish, scorpionfish, filefish, and the occasional puffer. Catch-and-release is fine.

It's beginner-friendly enough for kids and low-effort enough for grandparents. One side of the deck fishes, the other side drinks wine. Nobody has to do both.

Best Time to Sail (June–August)

Summer in Jeju means long days and late sunsets — usually around 7:30–7:45 pm in June and July. That means the sunset cruise leaves later, the light is golden for longer, and you still have time for dinner in Jungmun afterwards.

Book the sunset slot for the photos; pick the daytime luxury slot if you have small kids who'd rather not be out past bedtime. Either way, the 360-degree golden-hour view at the end of the loop happens once a day, weather permitting.

Is It Safe for Kids and Older Travellers?

The catamaran's twin-hull design is noticeably more stable than a monohull, which matters if anyone in your group gets seasick. A licensed captain and engineer are onboard every sailing, and Grande Bleu has been operating since 2011 with over 100,000 guests and long-running partnerships with the Shilla and Parnas hotels.

Boarding is step-on, step-off at the dock — no tenders, no ladders. Fine for grandparents, fine for a six-year-old.

How to Get There

Daepo Port is about 5 minutes by car from the Jungmun resort area and roughly an hour from Jeju International Airport. Arrive 15 minutes before departure for the safety briefing. No dress code, no bag drop — come as you are.
