Why Families Pick a Catamaran Over a Single-Hull

A catamaran has two hulls sitting wide apart, with a deck stretched between them. When a wave hits side-on, one hull braces the other — so the roll you'd normally feel on a single-hull (monohull) yacht is dramatically reduced.

That difference hits kids and older travellers harder than it hits a fit 30-year-old. Inner-ear balance isn't fully developed in young children, and seniors often feel rolling motion more sharply too. On Grande Bleu, you can sip a drink on deck without spilling it — that's the practical test.

The 60-minute route is also deliberately short. Long enough to see the coast properly, short enough that nobody's bored, queasy or sunburnt by the end. For a friends-and-family trip in autumn, when Jeju's October light is at its best, that's a sensible window.

Space to Move (Not Just Seat Numbers)

Capacity figures can be misleading. What matters is square metres per person, and a catamaran wins on that count because the wide bridging deck adds usable space a monohull simply doesn't have.

In practice:

  • Indoor saloon when the wind picks up
  • Open rear deck for sunbathing and photos
  • Bow seats up front for the panoramic shot
  • Room for kids to wander between all three without parents hovering

You're not perched on a narrow side rail trying to keep a toddler still. That alone is worth the ticket for grandparents travelling with younger family.

The Route: Daepo Port to Wolpyeong's Volcanic Coast

The boat heads west along Seogwipo's southern coastline. You'll pass, in order:

  • Wolpyeong Basalt Columns (Jusangjeolli) — hexagonal volcanic cliffs, part of the UNESCO Geopark zone
  • Elephant Rock (Kkokkiribawi) — looks exactly like an elephant's trunk, no explanation needed
  • Wolpyeong Sea Cave — waves crashing into a dark hollow in the cliff
  • Jingotnae Sea Canyon — sheer vertical cliffs dropping straight into water
  • Pangpaengideok — flat reef platform, the photogenic one
  • Keunbada Donggul (Large Sea Cave) — biggest cave on this stretch

You cannot see most of this from a car. The coastal road sits too high and too far inland. From the water, the volcanic geology that made Jeju a UNESCO site actually makes sense.

Fishing: Real Rods, Real Jeju Fish

Fishing gear is provided free. You'll likely hook rockfish (우럭), scorpionfish, filefish or small puffer — all genuine local species, not props. Beginners get help from the crew, and catch-and-release is fine if you'd rather not keep anything.

For kids, the moment they actually feel a fish on the line tends to outrank every theme park on the trip.

Best Time to Sail: Why Autumn Works

September to November is arguably Jeju's best window. Typhoon season is done, humidity drops, and the light gets that low-angle clarity that makes the basalt columns photograph properly. October sailings tend to book out fastest — worth pencilling in early rather than waiting on the day.
