Why a Catamaran Actually Matters (Skip if You Don't Get Seasick)

Single-hull motorboats — the kind you'll find on most Jeju sailing trips — cut through waves and rock side to side. After 20 minutes, your stomach remembers the trip more than your eyes do.

A catamaran has two hulls. Waves pass between them instead of slamming one side. The boat glides rather than bounces. That's why you can balance a glass of wine on the table, why grandparents can walk the deck without grabbing rails, and why your photos aren't blurry.

Grande Bleu is the only certified catamaran brand in Korea, which sounds like marketing fluff until you've tried both. For travelers in their 50s celebrating an anniversary — especially first-timers to Jeju Island — this difference is the whole game.

The Route: Six Coastal Landmarks in One Hour

The 60-minute loop leaves Daepo Port and tracks the volcanic coast west. You don't need to memorise the Korean names, but here's what you'll actually see:

  • Elephant Rock (Kkokkiribawi) — a cliff that genuinely looks like an elephant
  • Jingotnae Sea Canyon — vertical cliff walls dropping straight into the sea
  • Wolpyeong Basalt Columns — hexagonal volcanic pillars, part of Jeju's UNESCO Global Geopark
  • Wolpyeong Sea Cave — boat noses close to the entrance; waves crash inside
  • Pangpaengideok — a wide, flat reef plateau that photographs ridiculously well
  • Keunbada Donggul — the largest sea cave on this stretch of coast

Compared to a Bali sunset cruise (crowded, motorised, no geology) or a Greek island day-sail (gorgeous but five hours minimum), this is a tight, focused hour. Good ROI if you're backpacking and counting won.

The Free Food & Drinks Situation (No Catch)

This is where the Jeju budget travel maths gets interesting. Everything onboard is included, unlimited, no extra charge:

  • Korean draft beer (fresh brew)
  • Red and white wine
  • Jeju tangerine juice, fresh-squeezed
  • Bottled water
  • Local cookies and traditional snacks
  • Korean ramyeon, cooked onboard

For a backpacker tier price of around ₩48,000 (promo) on the daytime tour, you're getting a sit-down cruise with hot noodles and refillable wine. I've paid more for an airport sandwich.

The ramyeon detail matters more than it sounds. On a rocking monohull, no one's serving hot soup. On a catamaran, you eat it on the deck while looking at basalt cliffs.

Fishing, Included

Every tour comes with rods and bait. Common catches off Jeju's south coast: rockfish, scorpionfish, filefish, puffer. Beginner-friendly, catch-and-release if you'd rather not deal with it.

It's not the main event, but for a group of friends or an anniversary trip where one half loves fishing and the other half loves wine — both happen at the same time on the same boat. That's the slow side of K-culture you don't get from K-dramas.

Spring (March–May): The Underrated Window

Most blogs push July–August for Jeju sunset cruise trips. They're wrong for budget travellers. Spring in Jeju means:

  • Calm seas (catamaran feels even smoother)
  • Mild temperatures (15–20°C, no sweating on deck)
  • Cherry blossoms onshore, canola fields in April
  • Fewer Korean domestic tourists midweek
  • Cheaper Seoul–Jeju flights

May is the sweet spot: warm enough for shorts, cool enough that the golden hour light lasts properly. The 360-degree panoramic sunset happens once a day. Plan around it.
